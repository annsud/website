abstract: >-
  The use of air quality monitoring networks to inform urban policies is critical,
  especially where populations are exposed to unprecedented levels of air
  pollution. High costs, however, limit city governments' ability to deploy
  reference-grade monitors at scale — only 33 reference-grade monitors serve all of
  Delhi (1,500 sq km, 15 million residents). We describe a high-precision
  spatio-temporal prediction model that derives fine-grained pollution maps from
  two years of data from a low-cost network of 28 custom-designed portable sensors.
  Combining message-passing recurrent neural networks with conventional
  spatio-temporal geostatistics, the model achieves high accuracy despite high data
  variability and intermittent availability, predicting within 1-hour windows at
  9.4–10.5% mean absolute percentage error. These fine-grained maps offer a path to
  citizen-driven low-cost monitoring of hazardous urban air quality.

Government monitoring stations give a city-wide average, but the air on one street
can be far worse than the next. By blending Delhi's official network with a dense
layer of low-cost sensors, this work builds fine-grained pollution maps that reveal
local hotspots — a low-cost template for the kind of monitoring that effective
air-quality regulation depends on.
