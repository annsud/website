abstract: >-
  Localized pollution hotspots within a city — each emanating from sources with
  small coverage over space and time — can produce widely varying human exposure
  to poor air quality. We study pollution hotspots in Delhi over a 30-month period
  (May 2018 to November 2020), combining a coarse network of government
  air-quality monitoring stations with a fine network of low-cost monitors we
  installed to capture PM concentrations at much finer spatial resolution. We show
  that augmenting existing large-scale networks with low-cost handheld monitors —
  without significant additional cost — can substantially improve our
  understanding of the localized air pollution to which citizens are exposed
  daily: demonstrating the feasibility of finer-grained measurement, a
  classification of well-defined hotspot types, and the identification of real
  observed incidents.

Government monitoring stations give a city-wide average, but the air on one street
can be far worse than the next. By blending the official network with a dense layer
of cheap handheld sensors across Delhi, this work builds fine-grained pollution
maps that reveal local hotspots — a low-cost template for the kind of monitoring
that effective air-quality regulation depends on.
