abstract: 'Billions of people in developing countries have no electricity or an unreliable supply. We argue that a major cause is that electricity is often treated as a right rather than a private good, setting off a four-step vicious circle: a social norm that power is owed regardless of payment leads to widespread subsidies, theft and non-payment; distribution companies then lose money on each unit sold; to limit losses, they ration supply by restricting hours and access; and as the link between payment and supply is severed, customers’ incentives to pay erode further. The equilibrium is uneven, sporadic access that holds back growth — and escaping it requires changing the norms and contracts that govern power, not just adding capacity.'

This paper offers a framework for one of development's most stubborn puzzles: why
expanding generating capacity so often fails to translate into reliable power. The
argument is that the problem is institutional rather than purely technical. Once
electricity is treated as an entitlement, payment and supply come unlinked, and the
system settles into a low-level equilibrium of subsidies, losses and rationing that
no amount of new capacity alone will fix.
