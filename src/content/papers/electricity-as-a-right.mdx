abstract: >-
  This paper seeks to explain why billions of people in developing countries
  either have no access to electricity or lack a reliable supply. We present
  evidence that these shortfalls are a consequence of electricity being treated as
  a right and that this sets off a vicious four-step circle. In step 1, because a
  social norm has developed that all deserve power independent of payment,
  subsidies, theft, and nonpayment are widely tolerated. In step 2, electricity
  distribution companies lose money with each unit of electricity sold and in total
  lose large sums of money. In step 3, government-owned distribution companies
  ration supply to limit losses by restricting access and hours of supply. In step
  4, power supply is no longer governed by market forces and the link between
  payment and supply is severed, reducing customers' incentives to pay. The
  equilibrium outcome is uneven and sporadic access that undermines growth.

This paper offers a framework for one of development's most stubborn puzzles: why
expanding generating capacity so often fails to translate into reliable power. The
argument is that the problem is institutional rather than purely technical. Once
electricity is treated as an entitlement, payment and supply come unlinked, and the
system settles into a low-level equilibrium of subsidies, losses and rationing that
no amount of new capacity alone will fix.
