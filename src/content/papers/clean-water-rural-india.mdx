summary: 'A cluster-randomized trial in rural India testing how best to allocate clean drinking water — by price or by quota — and measuring what households will actually pay for it.'
abstract: >-
  How should scarce resources be allocated in developing countries? Weitzman
  (1977) highlights a trade-off between prices, which generate allocative
  efficiency, and quotas, which have desirable distributional consequences. In
  partnership with a private company, we implement a cluster-randomized trial
  supplying clean water to rural Odisha, India, to measure the relative
  effectiveness of different allocation mechanisms: discounts; a monthly quota;
  and an exchangeable quota, where unused allocation can be exchanged for cash. We
  measure the price elasticity of demand for clean water, the health effects of
  consuming it, and the extent to which liquidity constraints and intra-household
  inefficiencies reduce consumption. Treatment induces a high degree of uptake,
  with both quota groups obtaining the most water; adoption is driven by the
  extensive margin. That the discount and exchange groups face the same marginal
  incentive but show very different uptake implies the existence of strong
  liquidity constraints.

Should clean water be sold cheaply to everyone, or rationed by quota? Working with
a private supplier across 151 villages, we delivered treated water to rural homes
under different rules and watched what people chose. Take-up was high — but the
fact that households offered an identical marginal price behaved very differently
depending on whether they paid up front reveals just how binding liquidity
constraints are for the rural poor.
