## A natural sanitation service, switched off

For most of the twentieth century, India's vultures did an enormous, invisible job: as obligate scavengers, tens of millions of them stripped livestock carcasses clean within hours, keeping rotting carrion out of fields, rivers and streets. Then, in the mid-1990s, the cheap veterinary anti-inflammatory **diclofenac** came into wide use. It was lethal to vultures that fed on treated cattle, and within a decade their populations had crashed to near-extinction — one of the fastest declines of any bird on record.

### What the collapse did to people

That collapse is a natural experiment in what happens when a keystone species disappears. We compare districts that were historically well-suited to vultures with those that were not, before and after the die-off.

The result is stark. Without vultures to clear them, carcasses were instead dumped into waterways or left to feral dogs and rats — vectors that spread disease to humans far more readily. All-cause human death rates in the affected districts rose by **more than 4 percent**.

### Putting a price on it

Translating that excess mortality into economic terms, we estimate damages on the order of **$69 billion per year**. The number is a way of making something usually left out of the ledger visible: the loss of a wild species is not only an ecological event but a measurable cost borne by people. It is also a cautionary tale about how a single, well-intentioned product can ripple through an ecosystem and back onto human health.
