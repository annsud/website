## The story behind the paper

Cap-and-trade is a textbook idea: set a total limit on pollution, hand out permits, and let firms trade so that whoever can cut most cheaply does. It works in places like the United States and Europe. The open question — the one regulators across the developing world kept asking — was whether it could possibly work where monitoring is thin and enforcement is weak.

So we did not just study a market. **We built one**, together with the Gujarat Pollution Control Board: the world's first cap-and-trade scheme for *particulate* air pollution, covering industrial plants in the textile city of Surat. And we evaluated it the way you'd evaluate a drug — a randomized controlled trial, with some plants entering the market and others continuing under the status quo.

### What we found

The market worked. Plants in the scheme complied with their emissions permits **99% of the time**, while control plants under conventional command-and-control regulation met their limits at most **66% of the time**. Market plants also cut pollution by **20–30%** relative to control plants — and spent *less* on abatement than the status quo would have required.

### Why it mattered

A working pollution market in a low-enforcement setting is not just an academic result — it is a template other regulators can copy. The scheme has since informed conversations about extending emissions trading to other pollutants and other cities, and the continuous-monitoring infrastructure it relied on is now part of how India regulates industrial air pollution.
